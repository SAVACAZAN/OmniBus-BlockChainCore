**Audience:** End users, custody operators, auditors, regulators
**Source of truth:** `core/isolated_wallet.zig`, `core/transaction.zig`
**Last updated:** 2026-04-28

## 1. Why 5 mnemonics?

Most blockchains, including Bitcoin and Ethereum, use a single BIP-39 mnemonic to derive every key the user owns. A 12-word phrase produces one master seed; that seed produces every signing key, every receive address, every signature, for the entire lifetime of the wallet. This is convenient, and for a single-algorithm chain it is the industry standard.

OmniBus rejects that model on purpose.

OmniBus uses **five independent BIP-39 mnemonics per user**, one per cryptographic domain. None of the five derives from any of the others. None is mathematically related to any of the others. Each is generated by an independent call to the OS RNG (see `isolated_wallet.zig:218-219`, `crypto.random.bytes(&entropy)`).

The reason is **compartmentalization**, the same principle that drives multi-vault custody at private banks: if one compartment is breached, the other compartments stay sealed.

### Attack scenario: one mnemonic compromised

Assume an attacker obtains your **LOVE** mnemonic (the ML-DSA seed). What can the attacker do?

| Asset | Industry standard wallet (1 mnemonic, 5 keys) | OmniBus (5 mnemonics, 5 keys) |
|---|---|---|
| LOVE balance | Drained | Drained |
| OMNI balance | Drained (same seed) | Safe (different mnemonic) |
| FOOD balance | Drained (same seed) | Safe (different mnemonic) |
| RENT balance | Drained (same seed) | Safe (different mnemonic) |
| VACATION encrypted payloads | Decryptable (same seed) | Safe (different mnemonic) |
| Multi-chain BTC/ETH/SOL addresses | Drained (same seed) | Safe (those derive from OMNI only) |

In a single-mnemonic system, one breach equals total loss. In OmniBus, one breach equals one-fifth loss, and the other four wallets continue to operate normally.

### Why this matters for post-quantum

OmniBus carries one classical algorithm (ECDSA secp256k1) and four NIST-standardized post-quantum algorithms (ML-DSA, Falcon, SLH-DSA, ML-KEM). If a future cryptanalytic break, classical or quantum, breaks one of those five algorithms, the corresponding domain becomes recoverable by an attacker. In a single-mnemonic system, that single break would compromise everything. In OmniBus, only the one domain is at risk; the other four continue to function under their independent algorithms.

The attacker cannot pivot from one domain to another, because there is no shared secret to pivot through.

## 2. The 5 domains

OmniBus organizes the user's identity into five named domains, each with its own algorithm, address prefix, and intended role. The mapping is enforced in `isolated_wallet.zig:23-48`.

| Domain | Algorithm | Address prefix | NIST status | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| OMNI | secp256k1 ECDSA | `ob1q...` | Pre-quantum, FIPS 186-4 | Native OMNI coin transfers, multi-chain bridge addresses |
| LOVE | ML-DSA-87 (Dilithium-5) | `ob_k1_...` | FIPS 204 (PQC standard) | High-value PQ-signed transfers, long-horizon storage |
| FOOD | Falcon-512 | `ob_f5_...` | NIST PQC final-round (compact signatures) | Low-bandwidth signatures, IoT-grade clients |
| RENT | SLH-DSA-256s (SPHINCS+) | `ob_d5_...` | FIPS 205 (PQC standard) | Hash-based, no number-theoretic assumptions, archival signing |
| VACATION | ML-KEM-768 (Kyber) | `ob_s3_...` | FIPS 203 (PQC standard) | Encryption / key encapsulation, NOT signing |

**NIST citations.** ML-DSA is standardized as FIPS 204 (August 2024). SLH-DSA is standardized as FIPS 205 (August 2024). ML-KEM is standardized as FIPS 203 (August 2024). Falcon was selected in NIST PQC Round 3 and is on track for FIPS 206. ECDSA secp256k1 is the same curve used by Bitcoin since 2009.

**One special case.** VACATION uses ML-KEM, which is a key encapsulation mechanism, not a signature scheme. It cannot sign. It is used for encrypted-payload TXs where a sender encrypts to the recipient's VACATION public key and only the recipient can decrypt. The OmniBus chain rejects any attempt to sign with VACATION (see `isolated_wallet.zig:209-212`, `signVacation` returns `error.SchemeNotSignable`).

**Address prefix is binding.** The prefix is not cosmetic. The chain's verifier dispatcher reads the prefix, looks up the scheme via `Scheme.fromAddress` (`isolated_wallet.zig:54-61`), and routes the TX to the matching algorithm verifier. A signature produced by LOVE that arrives at an `ob1q...` address will be rejected, because the chain will run the OMNI ECDSA verifier on a Dilithium signature, and the verifier returns false.

## 3. Backup strategy

This section is the most important content in this document. **Lose a mnemonic, lose the funds.** OmniBus offers no recovery service, no social recovery, no admin override. The five mnemonics are the only existing copies of the only existing keys. Treat them accordingly.

### Required: 5 phrases, 3 physical locations each

For each of the five mnemonics, the user must produce at least **three physical copies** stored in **three geographically separated locations**. The recommended pattern follows industry guidance for cold storage of high-value assets:

- **Location 1:** Primary residence, fireproof safe.
- **Location 2:** Bank safe deposit box or trusted relative's home.
- **Location 3:** Off-site backup at a different city or country (geographic separation against natural disaster).

The three copies of one mnemonic are interchangeable. Recovering any one of the three is sufficient to restore that domain. The threat model is loss, not theft of a single copy; if any one location is compromised, the user must immediately move funds in that domain to a freshly generated wallet.

### Forbidden: digital storage without strong encryption

The following storage practices are **explicitly forbidden** for an OmniBus mnemonic, because they have all caused total losses for users of other chains:

- Photograph of the seed phrase on a phone.
- Plaintext file in cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud, OneDrive).
- Plaintext file in a password manager (some sync to clouds with weaker auth).
- Email to self, including drafts.
- SMS or messaging app history.
- Screenshot of the wallet UI showing the phrase.
- Any storage on a network-connected device that is not fully encrypted at rest with a password not also stored on that device.

If a digital copy is required for operational reasons, encrypt it with **age**, **GPG**, or an equivalent end-to-end cipher, store the cipher key offline, and document the recovery procedure to a custodian who can act if you cannot.

### Recommended backup checklist

For each of the five mnemonics, complete the following before transferring any value to that domain:

- [ ] Mnemonic written by hand on archival paper (acid-free, pencil or pigment ink, no thermal-printer paper).
- [ ] Copy 1 stored at primary residence in fireproof container.
- [ ] Copy 2 stored at off-site location 1 (bank or trusted relative).
- [ ] Copy 3 stored at off-site location 2 (geographically separated from copies 1 and 2).
- [ ] Domain label clearly written on each copy (e.g., "OMNI mnemonic — OmniBus", "LOVE mnemonic — OmniBus") so a recovering family member can identify the domain.
- [ ] Test restore performed with a small balance: send 0.001 OMNI to a fresh wallet, restore from the backed-up phrase, confirm the address matches and the balance is visible.
- [ ] No digital photograph, screenshot, or unencrypted file copy exists.
- [ ] Steel backup (Cryptosteel, Billfodl, or equivalent) for at least one of the three copies of each phrase.
- [ ] Estate-planning entry: a custodian is informed of the existence and location of the backups, with instructions, but not the phrases themselves.

For a user with five domains, this is **five complete backup ceremonies**, not one. The user is encouraged to perform them on five different days to avoid mental shortcuts (such as photographing all five at once in a moment of fatigue).

## 4. What you lose if you lose a mnemonic

The OmniBus chain treats a lost mnemonic as a permanent freeze on the funds in that domain. This section explains exactly what is lost, what is preserved, and why no recovery is possible.

### The funds in that domain are permanently inaccessible

If the LOVE mnemonic is lost, the LOVE address (`ob_k1_...`) and all balance at that address can no longer be spent. The chain still holds the balance forever; the address still exists in the UTXO set; the balance can still be queried via RPC; but no signing key exists to authorize a spend. The funds are functionally frozen.

There is no admin function to reassign the balance. There is no social-recovery quorum. There is no chain governance vote that can move the funds. The chain enforces "only the holder of the LOVE secret key can spend from `ob_k1_...`" as a cryptographic invariant, and that invariant is what makes the chain trustworthy. Breaking it for one user would break it for all users.

### What stays safe

If only the LOVE mnemonic is lost, and the other four are intact:

- **OMNI balance:** Safe. Different mnemonic, different secp256k1 key, no derivation link.
- **FOOD balance:** Safe. Different mnemonic, different Falcon key.
- **RENT balance:** Safe. Different mnemonic, different SLH-DSA key.
- **VACATION encrypted payloads:** Safe. Different mnemonic, different ML-KEM key.
- **Multi-chain BTC/ETH/SOL addresses:** Safe. They derive from OMNI only, not from LOVE.
- **Transaction history of all five domains:** Visible. The chain records all history publicly; loss of a mnemonic only blocks future spends.
- **Pq attestations (if previously published):** Visible. A `pq_attest` op_return is a chain artifact; it persists regardless of key loss.

The user retains complete operational use of the four surviving domains. Only the one lost domain is frozen.

### Concrete example

A user holds the following balances:

- OMNI: 100 OMNI
- LOVE: 50 OMNI
- FOOD: 0 OMNI (unused)
- RENT: 25 OMNI
- VACATION: encrypted documents only, no balance

The user loses the FOOD mnemonic. Result: nothing changes. FOOD held no balance. The other four domains continue normally.

The user loses the LOVE mnemonic. Result: 50 OMNI permanently frozen at the LOVE address. The remaining 125 OMNI (OMNI + RENT) and the VACATION encrypted documents are unaffected. The user can continue to transact from OMNI, RENT, and VACATION as before. Future incoming transfers to the LOVE address will accumulate at that address, also frozen.

The user loses all five mnemonics. Result: total loss of all balances, total loss of all encrypted documents, total loss of multi-chain addresses derived from OMNI. This is the catastrophic case the backup strategy in section 3 is designed to prevent.

### No recovery, by design

A reader familiar with custodial wallets may ask why OmniBus does not offer a recovery service. The answer is that any recovery service requires either (a) a copy of the user's secrets held by a third party, or (b) a chain-level mechanism for an authority to override the cryptographic invariant. Both of those are weaker security models than the one OmniBus provides. The chain is intentionally a trustless layer; the user is the only party with the keys; the user is the only party who can spend.

This is the same design as Bitcoin. It is not a bug.

## 5. Linking your wallets (opt-in)

By default the five wallets in a single user's identity are **on-chain unlinkable**. An external observer who sees a transaction from `ob1q...` and a transaction from `ob_k1_...` has no cryptographic evidence that these wallets belong to the same user. The five mnemonics are independent, the five addresses are derived independently, and there is no shared secret that a chain analyst can correlate.

This default-anonymous behavior is desirable for most users, especially those who want to keep their high-value LOVE wallet separate from their day-to-day OMNI wallet.

### When you might want to link

There are legitimate cases where the user wants to publish a verifiable link between two of their own wallets:

- A merchant publishes their OMNI receiving address publicly and wants customers to know that their LOVE address is also under the same ownership (so customers can pay in either).
- A regulated entity must demonstrate to an auditor that two addresses belong to the same legal person.
- A friend or family member needs to know your LOVE address but only knows your OMNI address.

For these cases OmniBus provides an opt-in mechanism: the **`pq_attest`** op_return.

### The `pq_attest` mechanism

The user signs and broadcasts a normal OMNI transaction. The transaction can be of any amount (including zero, if the chain rules allow zero-amount data TXs; see `transaction.zig:79`, "Unlike Bitcoin, OP_RETURN TXs with amount > 0 are allowed"). The transaction's `op_return` field carries the attestation payload:

```
pq_attest:<domain>:<pq_address>
```

For example, to publish that the user's LOVE address is `ob_k1_AAAA...`, the user sends an OMNI transaction with:

```
op_return = "pq_attest:love:ob_k1_AAAA..."
```

Because the op_return is included in the TX hash (`transaction.zig:196-199`), the attestation cannot be tampered with after the fact. Because the TX is signed by the OMNI key, only the holder of the OMNI mnemonic can publish an attestation from that OMNI address. This makes the attestation cryptographically binding.

### Querying attestations

Anyone (including the user's customers, auditors, or counterparties) can query the chain via JSON-RPC:

```json
{ "method": "pq_attestation", "params": ["ob1q...OMNI...", "love"] }
```

The response is either `null` (no attestation has been published) or:

```json
{
  "omni_address": "ob1q...",
  "domain": "love",
  "pq_address": "ob_k1_AAAA...",
  "tx_hash": "...",
  "block_height": 12345,
  "confirmations": 6
}
```

The `confirmations` field uses the same Bitcoin-style formula as ordinary transactions (`blockchain.zig:444-451`, `current_height - block_height`), so the querying party can apply their own confidence threshold (6 for soft finality, more for high-value flows).

### Attestations are public and permanent

A user who publishes a `pq_attest` op_return cannot un-publish it. The attestation lives in the chain forever, the same way a Bitcoin transaction's op_return lives forever. The user should consider whether they want this link to be public **before** signing the TX. There is no rollback.

A user who values default anonymity should simply **not publish an attestation**. The wallets remain unlinkable on-chain by default.

## 6. FAQ

### Q1: Can the chain link my 5 wallets without my permission?

**No.** The chain stores per-address state (balance, nonce, transaction history) keyed only by address. The five addresses of a single user have **no on-chain field that connects them**. The chain cannot derive your LOVE mnemonic from your OMNI mnemonic (they are independent), cannot infer common ownership from address patterns (the addresses derive from independent random entropy), and cannot perform cross-domain analysis (the addresses use different cryptographic algorithms with no shared structure).

The only way the link becomes visible is if the user explicitly publishes a `pq_attest` op_return, or if the user signs a multi-input transaction that spends from two domains in the same TX (which is not currently supported, and would be visible if it ever were).

### Q2: What if I want a friend to know my LOVE address?

Two options:

1. **Out of band.** Send the LOVE address to your friend via your normal trusted channel (signal, encrypted email, in person). The chain is not involved. The link is not published. This is what most users do most of the time.

2. **On-chain attestation.** Sign and broadcast a `pq_attest:love:ob_k1_...` op_return from your OMNI wallet. The link becomes public, verifiable by any chain observer, and permanent. This is recommended only when the link benefits from cryptographic auditability (merchants, regulated entities).

### Q3: What about quantum?

Four of the five domains are **quantum-resistant**.

- LOVE (ML-DSA): NIST FIPS 204 standard, lattice-based, believed to resist quantum attacks via Shor's algorithm and Grover's algorithm.
- FOOD (Falcon): NIST PQC final-round selection, lattice-based, similar quantum resistance.
- RENT (SLH-DSA): NIST FIPS 205 standard, hash-based, quantum resistance reduces to the security of SHA-256, which loses only half its bit-strength to Grover (256 bits classical → 128 bits quantum, still secure).
- VACATION (ML-KEM): NIST FIPS 203 standard, lattice-based KEM, quantum-resistant key encapsulation.

The fifth domain, OMNI (ECDSA secp256k1), is **not** quantum-resistant. A sufficiently large quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could derive an OMNI private key from a public key, given enough qubits and runtime. Estimates of when this becomes practical range from "never" to "decade-scale", depending on hardware progress.

OmniBus's strategy: keep ECDSA for compatibility with Bitcoin and Ethereum bridges, but encourage users to keep their long-horizon high-value funds in one of the four PQ domains. If a quantum break occurs, OMNI users would migrate funds out via a hash-time-locked atomic swap (the destination would be one of the user's PQ addresses), and the OMNI balance would be moved to safety before the public key becomes a target.

In the meantime, the user gets the best of both worlds: ECDSA compatibility for today, four independent PQ algorithms for tomorrow, no shared secret across the five domains.

### Q4: How do I check that my five mnemonics really are independent?

Run the cross-domain rejection test in `isolated_wallet.zig:445-464`. A LOVE-signed message that the chain attempts to verify under the OMNI verifier returns false. The same is true of every other algorithm pair. The chain enforces this by routing every TX through the algorithm matching the address prefix, with no fallback path.

A user who wants stronger evidence can dump the 32-byte entropy of two of their mnemonics and confirm that the bitwise XOR has popcount close to 128 (the expected difference between two random 256-bit values). Pattern-matched derivation would produce structured XORs (zeros, all-ones, or repeating blocks); independent generation produces noise.

### Q5: How is the 80-byte op_return per TX limit enforced?

Same as Bitcoin: hard cap declared in `transaction.zig:113`, `MAX_OP_RETURN: usize = 80`. Any TX with `op_return.len > 80` is rejected by `validateTransaction` before it ever reaches the mempool. The 80-byte limit is the same on all five domains. A user who needs more metadata bandwidth can spread it across multiple TXs from multiple domains: 80 bytes × 5 wallets = 400 bytes effective per identity per block, without ever co-signing a single TX.

### Q6: What is the relationship between OMNI and the multi-chain BTC/ETH/SOL addresses my wallet shows?

The multi-chain addresses are derived **only from the OMNI mnemonic**, because they require secp256k1, which is the algorithm OMNI uses. The four PQ domains do not have multi-chain addresses; they are OmniBus-native only.

If the user loses the OMNI mnemonic, they lose access to all multi-chain addresses derived from it. The four PQ wallets are unaffected.
